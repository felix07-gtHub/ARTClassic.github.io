Michelangelo Merisi da Caravaggio

1571–1610

A revolutionary Italian painter, redefined 17th-century art with his intense realism and dramatic use of chiaroscuro. Moving to Rome in 1576 to escape the plague, he apprenticed with Simone Peterzano and absorbed the influences of Renaissance masters like Leonardo da Vinci and Michelangelo. Caravaggio's works, including The Calling of Saint Matthew and The Crucifixion of Saint Peter, depicted religious and mythological scenes with everyday models, breaking from idealized traditions to achieve a striking immediacy. Despite a tumultuous life marked by frequent brawls and exile from Rome in 1606, Caravaggio's innovative style profoundly influenced artists like Rubens, Rembrandt, and Velázquez, securing his legacy as a transformative figure in Western art.

Saint Jerome Writing

Saint Jerome Writing

1605

Saint Jerome Writing, also referred to as Saint Jerome in His Study or simply Saint Jerome, is an oil painting by the Italian artist Caravaggio, typically dated between 1605 and 1606. It is displayed in the Galleria Borghese in Rome. The painting illustrates Saint Jerome, a Doctor of the Church in Roman Catholicism who has often been depicted by various artists, including Caravaggio. In this portrayal, Jerome is shown reading deeply, with one arm extended and resting on a quill. It has been suggested that he is engaged in the translation of the Vulgate, the Latin version of the Bible.

Saint Francis of Assisi in Ecstasy

Saint Francis of Assisi in Ecstasy

1595

This painting marks Caravaggio's first foray into religious art and is believed to have been created around 1595, shortly after he joined the household of Cardinal Francesco Maria Del Monte. It depicts Saint Francis of Assisi (the Cardinal's namesake) at the moment he receives the wounds of the Stigmata, which are the same marks left on Christ's body during the Crucifixion. The narrative is recounted by one of Francis' companions, Brother Leo. In 1224, Francis withdrew to the wilderness with a small group of his followers to reflect on God.

Cardsharps

Cardsharps

1594

The artwork depicts a well-dressed but naive boy engaged in a card game with another boy. The second boy, a con artist, has concealed extra cards tucked into his belt, hidden from the unsuspecting player but visible to the audience. A menacing older man looms behind the gullible boy, discreetly signaling to his young partner in crime. The second boy also has a dagger at his side, hinting that danger may be imminent.

Les Musiciens

Les Musiciens

1595

The picture shows four boys in Classical-style costumes. Three of them are playing musical instruments or singing. The fourth boy is dressed as Cupid and reaching for a bunch of grapes. The manuscripts indicate that the boys are practicing songs about love. The lutenist, who is the main figure, has tears in his eyes, suggesting that the songs express the sadness of love instead of its joys.

Martyrdom of Saint Matthew

Martyrdom of Saint Matthew

1599-1600

Cardinal Contarelli specifically requested this scene before he passed away, detailing that the painting should depict three key elements: a soldier murdering the saint, spectators exhibiting shock and disgust, and some architectural features in the background. An x-ray analysis of the painting reveals that Caravaggio experimented with two different compositions before finalizing the one we observe today, ultimately removing the architectural component altogether.

Rest on the Flight into Egypt

Rest on the Flight into Egypt

1597

Caravaggio depicts Mary sleeping with the infant Jesus, while Joseph holds a manuscript for an angel who is serenading Mary with a hymn played on the viol. This scene is not based on a specific event from the Bible, but rather on a series of legends that developed in the early Middle Ages surrounding the story of the Holy Family's escape into Egypt after being warned about King Herod's intention to kill the Christ Child.

Entombment

Entombment

1603

The Entombment is a dramatic depiction of the moment Christ's body is placed in the tomb. The scene captures the grief and agony of his followers. The work contrasts the living figure of Christ, depicted as lifeless and heavy, with the vigorous effort of those around him, such as the figures of Mary Magdalene and Nicodemus, who are shown supporting Christ's body.

Crucifixion of Saint Peter

Crucifixion of Saint Peter

1600

The Crucifixion of Saint Peter was painted by Caravaggio around 1600 and is one of his most intense religious works. The painting shows the apostle Peter being crucified upside down, a fate he chose to face because he felt unworthy of dying in the same manner as Christ. The realism and dramatic expression of the figures are characteristic of Caravaggio's early work.

Narcissus

Narcissus

1597

Narcissus was a handsome young man who, according to Greek mythology, became so enamored with his reflection in a pool of water that he stared at it until he died. Caravaggio's version of this myth shows Narcissus gazing at his reflection, his face framed in the water, and the painting emphasizes the tragic outcome of his self-obsession.

Conversion of Saint Paul

Conversion of Saint Paul

1601

The Conversion of Saint Paul depicts the moment when Saul, a persecutor of Christians, is struck blind by a divine light on his way to Damascus. He is shown in a dramatic moment of falling from his horse, experiencing the physical manifestation of God's intervention. Caravaggio's mastery of light and shadow gives the scene a powerful emotional impact, emphasizing the moment of divine grace and transformation.

Ecce Homo

Ecce Homo

1605

Ecce Homo, a Latin phrase meaning "Behold the Man," represents the moment when Jesus is presented by Pilate to the crowd before his crucifixion. In this painting, Caravaggio presents a strikingly realistic portrait of Jesus, wearing a crown of thorns, with blood trickling down his face. The figure of Jesus is framed by soldiers who mockingly gesture toward him, creating a scene filled with tension and pathos.

Boy with a Basket of Fruit

Boy with a Basket of Fruit

1593

Boy with a Basket of Fruit is one of Caravaggio's early works, featuring a young boy holding a basket filled with an assortment of fruits. The painting is an example of Caravaggio’s ability to capture the texture and detail of everyday objects, elevating them to the level of art. The boy’s expression is innocent, yet he appears to be aware of the viewer's gaze. The work highlights Caravaggio’s interest in realism, particularly in his depiction of light and shadow.
