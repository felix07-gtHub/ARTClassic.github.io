Leonardo da Vinci

1452–1519

Was an Italian polymath celebrated as one of the greatest minds of the Renaissance. He excelled in various fields, including painting, sculpture, anatomy, engineering, and invention. Known for his iconic works like Mona Lisa and The Last Supper, his art is renowned for its realism and emotional depth. Beyond art, Leonardo's detailed notebooks reveal his visionary ideas, including designs for flying machines and studies of human anatomy, showcasing his endless curiosity and genius.

Mona Lisa

Mona Lisa

1503-1506

Leonardo’s most famous painting, the "Mona Lisa," is renowned for its enigmatic smile and the subtle use of sfumato, a technique that blurs the lines between light and shadow. The sitter's expression is ambiguous, inviting endless interpretation. The background landscape, with its dreamy and almost surreal quality, adds to the painting's mystique. The work is a masterpiece of Renaissance portraiture, showcasing Leonardo’s deep understanding of human emotion and anatomy.

Saint John the Baptist

Saint John the Baptist

1513-1516

In this later work, Leonardo portrays Saint John the Baptist with an enigmatic, almost mystical presence. The figure is rendered in dark tones, with the saint’s pointed finger reaching out as if to invite reflection on his role as the herald of Christ. The use of light and shadow creates a dramatic contrast, enhancing the figure’s otherworldly aura. This piece is often seen as an exploration of the theme of spiritual revelation.

Salvator Mundi

Salvator Mundi

1499-1500

"Salvator Mundi," believed to be one of the last works by Leonardo, portrays Christ in a serene yet powerful stance, raising his right hand in blessing while holding a crystal orb in his left. The orb, symbolizing the cosmos, is rendered with extraordinary realism. This work combines religious symbolism with the artist's meticulous study of light, texture, and human form, showcasing his deep knowledge of both art and science.

St. Jerome in the Wilderness

St. Jerome in the Wilderness

1480

This unfinished work shows St. Jerome, a Christian scholar and translator, in a solitary, almost contemplative pose within a wilderness landscape. The sparse background emphasizes the figure’s isolation and dedication. The sketch-like quality of the piece is typical of Leonardo’s creative process, capturing the saint’s emotional state through dramatic use of light and shadow. The work is a powerful reflection on the themes of solitude and inner transformation.

The Battle of Anghiari

The Battle of Anghiari

1505 (uncompleted)

"The Battle of Anghiari" is one of Leonardo’s most famous, though unfinished, works, meant to be a grand fresco in the Palazzo Vecchio. It depicts a moment in the Battle of Anghiari, where soldiers are locked in intense combat. Leonardo’s detailed exploration of human emotion, movement, and anatomy is evident in the dramatic expressions and dynamic poses. Although the fresco was never completed, it remains an influential work in the history of Renaissance art.

The Vitruvian Man

The Vitruvian Man

1490

One of Leonardo’s most iconic works, "The Vitruvian Man" is a drawing based on the principles of Vitruvius, an ancient Roman architect. The image illustrates the ideal proportions of the human body, with the figure positioned inside a square and circle. This work exemplifies Leonardo's synthesis of art and science, showcasing his deep knowledge of anatomy and mathematical principles. It remains a symbol of the Renaissance ideal of humanism and balance.

Adoration of the Magi

Adoration of the Magi

1481

The "Adoration of the Magi" is an unfinished altarpiece by Leonardo da Vinci, depicting the Three Wise Men presenting gifts to the infant Jesus. The composition combines a dramatic use of light and shadow, with the figures placed in a pyramidal structure, emphasizing the theme of divine revelation. This work was commissioned for the Monastery of San Donato a Scopeto in Florence.

Bacchus

Bacchus

1510

"Bacchus" is one of Leonardo's earlier works depicting the Roman god of wine and revelry. This painting shows Bacchus holding a cup of wine and surrounded by playful motifs. It highlights Leonardo's mastery of fluid anatomy and psychological depth in character portrayal. The painting reflects Renaissance ideals of humanism and the natural world.

Benois Madonna

Benois Madonna

1478

The "Benois Madonna" depicts the Virgin Mary with the infant Jesus, highlighting Leonardo's early use of the pyramidal composition. The child holds a flower, symbolizing purity, while the calm expression of the Virgin demonstrates both tenderness and strength. This work is known for its soft, ethereal quality and careful rendering of light and shadow.

Ginevra de Benci

Ginevra de Benci

1474

"Ginevra de Benci" is one of Leonardo's early portraits and demonstrates his deep understanding of human psychology and light. The young woman is presented in profile, with an enigmatic expression that has intrigued art historians for centuries. The painting also incorporates the symbolic use of a juniper bush, a reference to Ginevra's name and virtue.

La Belle Ferronnière

La Belle Ferronnière

1490

"La Belle Ferronnière" is one of the most famous portraits attributed to Leonardo. The painting depicts a woman wearing a headdress and a simple, elegant dress. The subject's serene expression, combined with her subtle gaze and soft modeling of light, exemplifies Leonardo's mastery of chiaroscuro and his focus on emotional depth.

Leda and the Swan

Leda and the Swan

1508

"Leda and the Swan" is one of Leonardo's most mythological works, depicting the Greek myth of Zeus transforming into a swan and seducing the mortal Leda. The work demonstrates Leonardo’s exploration of anatomy, gesture, and human emotion. Though the painting is lost, the composition and the delicate rendering of the figures have been captured in numerous copies and studies.
