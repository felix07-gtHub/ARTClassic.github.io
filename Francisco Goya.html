Francisco Goya

1746–1828

Francisco Goya was a prominent Spanish painter and printmaker, often regarded as one of the most important and influential artists of the late 18th and early 19th centuries. His work bridges the transition between the Old Masters and modern art, reflecting both the grandeur of his time and its darker aspects.

Charles IV of Spain and His Family

Charles IV of Spain and His Family

1800

This monumental family portrait by Francisco Goya depicts King Charles IV of Spain and his family. The painting showcases the royal family in a highly personal, almost intimate light. Goya portrays them in a setting that emphasizes both their royal status and human qualities, breaking away from the usual formal, idealized depictions of monarchs. Each family member is depicted with a sense of realism that reveals their personalities.

Portrait of Doña Isabel de Porcel

Portrait of Doña Isabel de Porcel

1805

Francisco de Goya’s ‘Portrait of Doña Isabel de Porcel’ was among the first paintings by the Spanish artist to enter the National Gallery in 1896. This portrait is heralded as one of Goya’s most dazzling works, capturing the subject's elegance and poise. However, its flamboyant style has led to debates regarding its attribution to Goya, with some scholars casting doubts over its authenticity.

Saturn Devouring His Son

Saturn Devouring His Son

1821-1823

This haunting work by Goya depicts the mythological story of Saturn, who devoured his own children to prevent them from overthrowing him. Painted during Goya's time of personal isolation, it is a powerful reflection on old age, tyranny, and human brutality. The darkness of the painting’s mood mirrors the turmoil of the artist’s own life, as well as the horrors of war.

Self-Portrait with Doctor Arrieta

Self-Portrait with Doctor Arrieta

1820

This deeply personal painting by Goya depicts the artist himself, gravely ill, supported by his doctor, Arrieta, who saved his life during a critical illness. The dark and shadowy figures in the background add a sense of foreboding to the scene, emphasizing the gravity of Goya’s condition. The work is a poignant tribute to the doctor and an exploration of Goya's mortality.

The Clothed Maja

The Clothed Maja

1800-1805

A counterpart to Goya’s earlier painting, The Nude Maja, this painting features the same woman, fully clothed in elegant attire. The juxtaposition of the two paintings highlights the themes of sensuality and modesty, exploring the contrasts in human nature. The Clothed Maja was as controversial as its nude counterpart, challenging traditional depictions of women in art.

The Colossus

The Colossus

1808-1812

The Colossus is a monumental painting that depicts a giant figure towering over a desolate landscape. The figure is a symbol of power, or possibly the looming threat of war. This work is thought to be an allegory of the terror and uncertainty during the Napoleonic invasion of Spain, with the giant representing the overwhelming force of war.

The Disasters of War

The Disasters of War

1810-1820

The Disasters of War is a harrowing series of prints by Goya, depicting the brutal and violent realities of the Peninsular War. The graphic depictions of suffering, death, and devastation are a stark commentary on the horrors of conflict. The series was not published during Goya’s lifetime due to its political nature and the shocking realism of the images.

The Dog

The Dog

1819-1823

The Dog is one of the most iconic works of Goya’s Black Paintings, depicting a small dog’s head peering upward from a vast, undefined space. The minimalist composition and lack of context suggest themes of isolation, helplessness, and despair. The painting’s ambiguity has led to various interpretations, including reflections on Goya’s own sense of alienation during his later years.

The Nude Maja

The Nude Maja

1797-1800

The Nude Maja is one of Goya's most famous works, depicting a reclining woman in a provocative pose. It is notable for its direct and unapologetic sensuality, which sparked considerable controversy in its time. Goya’s painting is often regarded as a precursor to modern art’s more liberated depictions of the female body.

The Sleep of Reason Produces Monsters

The Sleep of Reason Produces Monsters

1799

This etching by Goya is one of his most iconic works, showcasing a dreamlike and haunting vision of the artist’s own fears. It depicts a man asleep, surrounded by terrifying creatures that represent the consequences of abandoning reason. The image is often interpreted as a commentary on the chaos and irrationality that characterized Spain during Goya’s lifetime.

The Third of May 1808

The Third of May 1808

1814

This powerful and emotional painting by Goya commemorates the atrocities committed during the Peninsular War. It depicts the execution of Spanish civilians by French soldiers. The work is celebrated for its dramatic use of light and shadow, as well as its stark portrayal of human suffering and violence.

Witches' Sabbath

Witches' Sabbath

1797-1798

This eerie painting by Goya depicts a dark gathering of witches and a goat-headed figure, perhaps representing Satan. The painting’s disturbing atmosphere, combined with Goya’s masterful use of light and shadow, evokes a sense of fear and superstition, themes that were pervasive in the period.
